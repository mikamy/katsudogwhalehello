Understanding the Layoffs and Job Market Trends

A COMP6234 Data Visualisation Data Story

Introduction

The labour market has seen a significant increase in layoffs across multiple industries this year. To emphasize the impact of this, approximately 130000 employees have lost their jobs in 2022 alone. By definition, a layoff is defined as the downsizing or permanent termination of employment of an employee. To understand this sudden increase in downsizing, this data story was created to walk the reader through the history of the world’s labour market (dating back to the early 2000’s) and to give the reader an intuition of the layoffs and which industries are affected most.

What is the Great Resignation?


Beginning Spring 2021 in the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic, a large number of workers have been voluntarily quitting their jobs at higher-than-usual rates. This period is known to be the "Great Resignation" and is constantly being debated on by economists, analysts, and even the fellow hobbyist. Although there have been a lot of discussions and opinions in regards to whether or not this strange economic trend has only started to take place in recent years, it is more beneficial to study the trends over time rather than focusing too much on the meaning behind the buzzword. To do this, we will take a look at the quit rates, along with some other metrics to reveal the underlying trends behind the data.

An overview of the employee quit rates over the years.

A key metric that is used in reporting on the Great Resignation and employment market is the "quit rate". This metric is generated by the Job Opening and Labour Turnover Survey (JOLTS) programme of the Bureau of Labour Statistics (BLS). Looking at the bar chart below, it can be seen that prior to March/April 2020, the monthly quits have been fairly stable, until it hit a low point of 1.6% in April 2020. This is in line with the announcement of the COVID-19 pandemic, where employees were concerned of their job security levels. However in early 2021, the quit rates quickly returned to its pre-pandemic levels. The quit rate rose to 3.0% in November, reaching the highest level in decades. This amounts to more than four million people leaving their jobs in that month alone.
